Why we started redesigning AI in databases

By mid-2023, Notion AI had been generally available for a few months. Adoption of the writing assistant inside pages was strong — but the data told a different story for AI inside databases.

AI properties (AI Summary, AI Translation, AI Custom Autofill, etc.) had high first-use numbers and a steep drop-off after that. Many users tried it once, got a confusing result or didn't understand what just happened, and never came back.

That mattered. AI in databases was the surface where Notion's AI bet really showed its value — turning a workspace into something that could think across hundreds of rows, not just help write one paragraph. If users couldn't get past the first awkward interaction, the broader AI story was in trouble.

Internal data showed a 67% drop between first AI property creation and the second use within 14 days.

The workstream kicked off in April 2023. I joined Notion's team as an embedded designer through a partner agency.

How I tackled the problem

1 Dogfooding

Living inside the feature for a week

Before opening Figma, I spent a full week using AI properties for real work. I rebuilt my own task tracker, a content calendar, and a research database — all with AI properties doing real jobs: summarizing meeting notes, translating Arabic content, categorizing tickets.

I kept a running log of every moment I got confused, frustrated, or surprised. I shared a Notion page with the Notion design team and asked three other designers to do the same. By the end of the week we'd captured 47 friction points across four designers.

Dogfooding log — week 1. 47 friction points captured across 4 designers, surfacing problems no analytics dashboard would have caught.

2 Quantitative analysis

Mapping the actual drop-off funnel

Once the PM and Data Analyst joined, we mapped the funnel across five stages: Discovery → Configuration → First fill → Review → Repeat use. The biggest cliffs were between Configuration → First fill (users abandoned mid-setup) and First fill → Repeat use (one-and-done behavior).

3 User interviews

22 interviews across three segments

We interviewed existing Notion AI subscribers, Notion users who hadn't bought AI, and prospects evaluating Notion against Coda and ClickUp. I ran the sessions remotely — asking people to set up an AI property in their own workspace while talking through what they expected. The screen-share format surfaced hesitation and confusion in real time.

Remote research sessions — 22 interviews, 3 segments. Watching users set up AI properties in real workspaces surfaced hesitation analytics never would.

What we found — three core frictions

01
Finding 01
Invisible state

When a user triggered an AI property, the cell showed a small spinner — or sometimes nothing at all if the row was off-screen. Users had no idea whether the AI was running, queued, failed, or done. Several testers refreshed the page mid-generation, killing the request.

"Did it work? I genuinely can't tell. Is it still doing it? I'll just… wait, I guess."

Prospect, 8 minutes into a 12-minute session
02
Finding 02
No mental model for "what is the AI looking at?"

AI properties pull from other properties in the same row — but the UI never told users that. When the AI returned something irrelevant or hallucinated, users blamed the AI itself rather than realising the source column was empty or wrong. Once a user got a bad output they couldn't explain, they stopped trusting the feature entirely. This was the trust killer.

03
Finding 03
Destructive-feeling actions with no safety net

The "Re-generate" button replaced the existing AI output instantly with no undo affordance. Users hovered, hesitated, and often didn't click — preferring to leave a bad output in place rather than risk losing it. Configuration changes had the same problem: editing the prompt silently invalidated every row in the database.

And a few more issues we found along the way

  • AI properties were buried below 14 other property types in the picker, with no visual distinction.
  • The prompt configuration UI used the same modal as regular property settings — giving no signal that what you type here will run on every row.
  • Error states said "Something went wrong" with no information about what went wrong or whether to retry.
  • Translated outputs didn't indicate source language, so non-English users couldn't verify the AI understood the input.

So how did we solve it?

Design sprint — prioritising as a team

I facilitated a two-day design sprint with the PM, EM, engineers, researcher, and data analyst. We mapped every friction point onto a 2×2 of user impact vs implementation cost and committed to a shared shortlist. From the workshop, we landed on four design bets.

  • 1
    Make AI state legible
    Clear, persistent indicators for generating, ready, stale, and failed states — at both the cell and column level.
  • 2
    Show the AI its homework
    Surface which source properties the AI is reading from, inline — as clickable pills that highlight the source columns.
  • 3
    Protect the user from themselves
    Non-destructive re-generation with version history. Previous output preserved as a one-tap undo.
  • 4
    Differentiate AI properties visually
    Give them their own affordance in the property picker and in the column header — distinct from the 14 other types.

Two-day design sprint. Seven people, one 2×2, four design bets. The framing question: what does a user need to trust an AI output?

Testing concepts with users

I built four mid-fidelity prototypes — one per bet — and tested them with eight users across two rounds. Key learnings: the source-property pill was the single highest-impact change, immediately showing users why outputs were good or bad. Persistent column-level state indicators helped users see at a glance that "all 47 rows are up to date." And inline re-generate with side-by-side comparison made users willing to actually click the button for the first time.

Final designs and what we shipped

1. A new AI property picker

+62%

We grouped all AI property types into a dedicated section at the top of the picker, with a one-liner describing what each one does and an example output preview on hover. Before: AI Summary buried alphabetically between "Files" and "Last edited time." After: its own section, scannable in two seconds.

+62% increase in successful AI property creation from the picker — fewer users abandoning mid-setup.

Before/after: the property picker. Moving AI properties to a dedicated section cut time-to-creation by over a third.

2. Source-property pills

+34%

Every AI property column now shows, inline, which properties it reads from. Click the pill and the source columns highlight across the entire database. Users could finally understand why an output looked the way it did — and started editing source columns instead of deleting AI outputs.

+34% increase in AI output edit-and-keep rate versus delete-and-retry.

3. State indicators at column level

−41%

A small status pill on the column header tells users: "47 of 47 ready," "12 generating," "3 stale — source changed," or "2 errors." Users no longer had to scroll every row to understand what the AI had done. At a glance: done, in progress, or broken.

−41% drop in "is the AI broken?" support tickets in the quarter post-launch.

4. Non-destructive re-generation

+78%

Re-generating now shows the new output side-by-side with the previous one. Users pick which to keep. Previous versions are stored for 7 days. The old one-click overwrite that scared users away from ever improving an output was gone — replaced with a simple comparison that felt safe.

+78% increase in re-generation clicks — users finally felt safe trying again.

Reflections

A few things I took away from this project that I've carried into every AI design problem since:

  • 01
    AI usability is mostly about state and provenance

    Users don't need to understand the model — they need to understand what it's reading, what it's doing right now, and how to undo it. Once those three things were legible, trust followed almost automatically.

  • 02
    Dogfooding catches things analytics never will

    The "is it broken or just slow?" problem was invisible in the funnel data. It only showed up when I sat with the feature for a week. You can't design for confusion you haven't experienced.

  • 03
    Trust is the real metric

    Adoption numbers were the headline, but the deeper win was that users started treating AI properties as a dependable part of their workflow — not a magic trick they tried once. Trust, once earned, compounds.
