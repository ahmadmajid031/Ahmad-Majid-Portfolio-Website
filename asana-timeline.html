Why we started redesigning the Timeline

By early 2024, Asana's Timeline view had been around for years — a Gantt-style planning surface meant to be the home base for project managers. On paper, it was one of the most differentiated views in the product. In reality, it was one of the least used.

List view dominated. Board view came second. Timeline sat in fourth place behind Calendar, despite being the view most aligned with how project managers said they thought about their work. Most teams created a Timeline once during project setup, looked at it for thirty seconds, and never opened it again.

That mattered. Timeline was the surface where Asana's promise — "see how the work connects" — was supposed to come alive. If users couldn't get value from it in the first session, the whole story about dependencies, critical paths, and cross-team coordination collapsed back into a flat list of tasks.

Internal data showed a 71% drop between first Timeline open and the second open within 14 days.

The workstream kicked off in January 2024. I joined Asana's team as an embedded designer through a partner agency.

How I tackled the problem

1 Dogfooding

Living inside the feature for a week

Before opening Figma, I spent a full week running real projects in Timeline view. I set up a content production schedule, a hiring pipeline with dependencies, and a marketing launch plan with three workstreams converging on one date. I forced myself to plan, replan, and report from Timeline only — no falling back to List.

I kept a running log of every moment I got stuck, lost context, or had to leave Timeline to do something basic. By the end of the week I'd captured 39 friction points. I shared the doc with two other designers on the Asana team and asked them to do the same.

Dogfooding log — week 1. 39 friction points captured across 3 designers, including problems no analytics dashboard would have caught: how often you scroll horizontally, how often you lose your place, how often you give up and switch back to List.

2 Quantitative analysis

Mapping the actual drop-off funnel

Working with the PM and researcher, we mapped Timeline usage across five stages: Discovery → First open → First edit → Dependency creation → Repeat use. The biggest cliffs were between First open → First edit (users looked, but didn't touch) and First edit → Dependency creation (users moved bars around but never connected them, missing the whole point of the view).

3 User interviews

18 sessions across three segments

We interviewed active Timeline users, Asana users who stuck with List view, and project managers evaluating Asana against Monday and ClickUp. I ran sessions remotely and asked people to open one of their real projects in Timeline and walk me through what they were looking at. The "walk me through what you see" framing surfaced something important: most users couldn't read their own Timeline. They knew tasks were there, but couldn't tell what was on track, what was at risk, or what was blocking what.

Remote research sessions — 18 interviews, 3 segments. Watching project managers try to read their own Timelines was the turning point. The problem wasn't features. It was legibility.

What we found — three core frictions

01
Finding 01
Visual noise without hierarchy

Every task bar looked the same. A two-hour design review and a six-week engineering build had identical visual weight. Color was tied to assignee or custom field — which meant a Timeline with 80 tasks looked like a confetti explosion. Users couldn't scan it. They had to read every bar to understand anything.

"I open this and my eyes just… slide off. I don't know where to start looking."

Project manager, 4 minutes into a 15-minute session
02
Finding 02
Dependencies were invisible until you knew to look for them

Dependencies in Timeline showed as thin gray arrows between bars. On a dense project, the arrows disappeared into the background. Worse — there was no way to see just the critical path. Users either saw every dependency as visual clutter, or none at all. The single most valuable thing Timeline could tell you — "if this task slips, here's what else slips" — was buried.

This was the trust killer. Once a deadline slipped because a user missed a dependency Timeline technically showed them, they stopped trusting the view to surface what mattered.

03
Finding 03
Editing felt destructive

Dragging a task bar to extend its duration silently shifted every dependent task downstream. No preview. No "this will affect 8 other tasks" warning. Users dragged once by accident, broke their plan, hit undo, and never dragged again. Most editing happened back in the task detail panel — defeating the purpose of a draggable Timeline.

And a few more issues we found along the way

  • Timeline had no concept of "today" beyond a thin vertical line — users lost their place constantly when scrolling.
  • Zoom levels (day/week/month/quarter) reset every time you switched views, forcing users to re-zoom on every visit.
  • Milestones looked almost identical to regular tasks despite being conceptually different.
  • There was no way to collapse a section or workstream — large projects became a wall of bars with no way to focus.
  • Filtering existed but lived three clicks deep in a menu most users never opened.

So how did we solve it?

Design sprint — prioritising as a team

I facilitated a two-day design sprint with the PM, EM, engineers, and researcher. We mapped every friction point onto a 2×2 of user impact vs implementation cost and committed to a shared shortlist. From the workshop, we landed on four design bets.

  • 1
    Build visual hierarchy into the bars themselves
    Differentiate task types, milestones, and parent rollups visually. Make status — on track, at risk, blocked, done — the primary signal, not assignee color.
  • 2
    Make dependencies and the critical path first-class
    Surface the critical path as a togglable layer with strong visual treatment. Let users isolate "what depends on this task" with one click.
  • 3
    Protect the user from themselves
    Drag-to-reschedule shows a live preview of every affected downstream task before commit. Undo is one keystroke and visible.
  • 4
    Give the Timeline a sense of place
    Sticky "today" indicator, persistent zoom level per project, collapsible swimlanes, and inline filtering at the top of the view.

Two-day design sprint. Six people, one 2×2, four design bets. The framing question: what does a project manager need to read a Timeline in under ten seconds?

Testing concepts with users

I built four mid-fidelity prototypes — one per bet — and tested them with seven users across two rounds. Key learnings: the critical-path toggle was the single highest-impact change, with every tester saying "I would actually use this on Monday." The drag preview made users willing to edit directly on the bars for the first time. The status-driven color system turned 80-task projects from confetti into something readable in seconds.

Final designs and what we shipped

1. A new visual language for task bars

+44%

We rebuilt the bar system around status as the primary signal. On track is a calm blue, at risk is amber, blocked is red, done is a muted fill with a checkmark. Milestones became diamond markers anchored to a date rather than spanning bars. Parent tasks rolled up child status visually — a parent bar with three at-risk children shows an amber edge.

+44% increase in users correctly identifying at-risk work within 10 seconds of opening Timeline, measured in usability testing.

Before/after: the task bar system. Status-driven color replaced assignee-driven color as the primary visual signal. Project managers could finally scan.

2. Critical path as a first-class layer

+58%

A toggle in the Timeline header lights up the critical path — every task whose slip would push the project end date. Non-critical tasks fade back. Click any task and "show what depends on this" highlights the downstream chain. Dependencies got thicker, color-coded by status, and routed around bars instead of through them.

+58% increase in dependency creation per project — users started building real dependency networks instead of flat task lists.

3. Drag with preview

+81%

Dragging a bar now shows a ghosted preview of every downstream task that will move, with a count: "This will reschedule 8 tasks." Users commit or cancel. Undo is one keystroke and a toast confirms what was reverted. The old silent cascade was gone.

+81% increase in direct-on-Timeline edits — users finally trusted the drag handle.

4. A Timeline that remembers where you were

−37%

Sticky "today" marker that follows you as you scroll. Zoom level persists per project. Swimlanes collapse and stay collapsed. Filters moved to a visible bar at the top of the view, with active filters shown as chips.

−37% drop in Timeline-related support tickets in the quarter post-launch.

Reflections

A few things I took away from this project that I've carried into every planning-tool problem since:

  • 01
    Power features fail on legibility, not capability

    Timeline had every feature a project manager could ask for. It failed because users couldn't read it in ten seconds. Once status became the primary visual signal, every other feature in the view got more valuable.

  • 02
    Dogfooding catches things analytics never will

    The "I lose my place when I scroll" problem was invisible in the funnel data. It only showed up when I tried to use Timeline for a week of real work. You can't design for friction you haven't felt.

  • 03
    Trust is the real metric

    Adoption numbers were the headline, but the deeper win was that project managers started treating Timeline as their home view — not a meeting-time artifact. A planning tool that you don't trust to edit isn't a planning tool. It's a screenshot.
